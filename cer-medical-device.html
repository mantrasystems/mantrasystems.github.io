Writing a Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) for the Medical Device Regulation

A Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) is an important technical document that must be produced for every medical device under the MDR. A CER details the process, findings and conclusions of clinical evaluation, and must be written in accordance with the rules for clinical evaluation in Annex XIV Part A of the Medical Device Regulation (MDR) 2017/745.

The MDR — fully in force from May 2021 — replaces the Medical Device Directive (MDD) and imposes a wide range of new regulatory requirements. Among the many changes is a new framework for performing clinical evaluation with updated requirements for writing a medical device CER. Writing a CER for the Medical Device Regulation is a specialised task that greatly benefits from the involvement of an expertly-trained medical professional.

What are the requirements for writing a CER under the Medical Device Regulation?

Article 61 of the Medical Device Regulation states that the clinical evaluation of each medical device shall be documented in a CER that must form part of the technical documentation portfolio for the device. Annex XIV Part A provides detailed guidance for performing a clinical evaluation, stating that every medical device CER must:

  • identify all clinical data relevant to the medical device, whether favourable or not
  • appraise clinical data using a robust, objective and documented methodology
  • identify any gaps in the clinical evidence portfolio
  • reach objective conclusions about the safety and performance of the medical device
  • make a determination of the benefit-risk profile of the device acounting for all available evidence

What other information sources are available?

More detailed guidance on CER writing can be found guidance for medical device manufacturers published by the European Commission. MEDDEV 2.7/1 rev 4 “Clinical Evaluation: A guide for manufacturers and notified bodies” provides a useful section on CER structure. However, it should be noted that the guideline has not yet been updated to reflect changes in requirements introduced by the MDR. Because of this, adherence to MEDDEV 2.7/1 rev 4 alone will not be sufficient to ensure that a CER meets Medical Device Regulation standards. Medical device manufacturers are advised to consult externally for expert advice in order to ensure that MDR requirements are met.

What happens to a medical device CER after completion?

Following completion, a CER will be assessed as a component of the technical documents required by Annex II MDR to be submitted in support of the medical device. The procedure by which this assessment will take place will vary according to the risk classification of the device, with a greater degree of regulatory scrutiny being required for higher risk devices.

In the case of a Class IIa, IIb or III medical device the CER must be submitted directly to a notified body for regulatory scrutiny as a pre-requisite for achieving permission to affix a CE-mark to the device. The MDR outlines responsibilities for notified bodies in performing assessments of clinical evaluation reports, requiring notified bodies to have access to individuals with sufficient scientific and technical expertise to make a reasoned assessment of the CER.
