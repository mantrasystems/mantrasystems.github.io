Software as a medical device — an MDR guide

Unless you have spent time working with medical device legislation in the past, the idea that software could be a medical device may be rather unexpected — a medical device is perhaps more traditionally viewed as a hardware product. Nonetheless, it is the case.

The new Medical Device Regulation (MDR) 2017/745 specifically includes provisions relating to software as a medical device. If your software performs a medical function then it is likely that it will be subject to the full scope of the MDR after May 2021. It is therefore vital that all manufacturers of medical software know whether their products will be classed as a medical device, and understand their range of responsibilities under the MDR.

Is my software product a medical device?

Not all software associated with the provision of healthcare will be classified as a medical device. Medical device legislation is intended to apply only to software whose expected purpose is in line with the definition of “medical device” provided in Article 2 of the MDR.

Software must have a medical purpose on its own in order to be classified as a medical device. This is important to consider whether the product in question functions as ‘stand-alone’ software (such as a diagnostic app), or whether it is an integral part of, or associated with, a hardware device.

Secondly, it is important to consider the extent to which medical value is added to data entered into the software. For example, simple archive and retrieval software that stores serial blood sugar values without manipulating the data would not normally be classed as a medical device. However, if that same software analysed the data and highlighted any worrying trends (perhaps by highlighting when a hospital visit may be needed) this is likely to be held as medical value and would mean that the software would be classified as a medical device.

What needs to be done if software is a medical device?

If a software product is classified as a medical device, then the full range of provisions in the Medical Device Regulation 2017/745 (“The MDR”) will apply to the product. This means that the software producer must implement a number of systems and processes, including:

  • Quality Management System
  • Post-Market Surveillance (PMS) system to monitor performance of the product
  • Post-market Clinical Follow-up and Vigilance systems as components of PMS
  • Performing a Clinical Evaluation of the product and submitting a Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) to regulatory authorities

It will also be necessary for software producers to understand how to work with MDR Annex VIII to determine risk classification for the product, as well as learning how to use MDR Annex I that lists General Safety and Performance Requirements that must be adhered to.

The majority of these requirements will be unfamiliar to many software manufacturers, making working with the MDR a daunting prospect.
